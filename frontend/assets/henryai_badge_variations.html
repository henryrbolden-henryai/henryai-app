HenryAI Badge & Seal Variations

Exploring the circular seal format with different approaches to text and geometry.

Option 1: Clean Badge, No Text
Just the H-Mark in a gradient circle. Maximum impact. Text lives outside when needed, or the badge stands completely alone.
Best for: App icons, social avatars, quality seals.
Option 2: Perimeter Text (Top Arc)
Subtle branding curved along the top edge. Luxury watch aesthetic. The mark is the hero, the text is contextual.
Best for: Premium positioning, certification badges, official seals.
Option 3: Mission Statement Perimeter
"STRATEGIC" above, "JOB SEARCH" below. Mission statement wrapped around the mark.
Best for: When you need to communicate what you do at a glance. Educational contexts, presentations.
Option 4: Hexagon Format
Tech geometry. More structure than a circle, still clean. Suggests precision and systematic thinking.
Best for: Tech/SaaS positioning, developer tools aesthetic, product badges.
Option 5: Shield Format
Protection. Guidance. Trust. Classic emblem shape that suggests you're defending candidates against a brutal market.
Best for: "Trusted advisor" positioning, security/protection messaging, premium service tier badges.
Option 6: Gradient Ring, Clean Center
Reversed emphasis. The gradient frames the mark instead of containing it. Works beautifully on light backgrounds.
Best for: Light mode interfaces, print materials, email signatures.
Option 7: Minimal Ring, Maximum Impact
Aggressive. The H-Mark dominates, the ring just contains it. More energy, less formal.
Best for: Bold brand personality, younger demographic, when you want the mark to feel powerful rather than refined.
Option 8: Double Ring Detail
Extra refinement. Two concentric rings add visual sophistication without bulk. Premium watch aesthetic.
Best for: High-end service tiers, executive positioning, when you want maximum polish.
Option 9: Octagon Format
Distinctive geometry. More corners than a hexagon, still symmetrical. Stops the scroll.
Best for: Standing out in crowded feeds, app stores, when circles and squares feel too common.
